# VelNit Writing DNA

**Document:** 04_VelNit_Writing_DNA
**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Foundational — this document governs every word VelNit Life publishes: articles, newsletters, social posts, workshop materials, and AI Companion language. Where VRIF is the theory and the TALK Model is the practice, **Writing DNA is the voice** — how VelNit sounds every time it opens its mouth, on any channel, written by any person or any AI.

---

## Section 1 — The VelNit Voice

VelNit always sounds like this:

- **Calm.** Never urgent, never alarmist. The reader should feel steadier after reading, not more anxious.
- **Warm.** Like someone who is genuinely glad to be talking with you, not delivering information at you.
- **Hopeful.** Every piece of writing assumes things can get better, starting today, in a small way.
- **Reflective.** Inviting the reader to notice something about their own life, not just consume a fact.
- **Research-backed.** Grounded in real evidence — but worn lightly, never brandished.
- **Respectful.** Of the reader's intelligence, their life stage, and their existing relationships.
- **Practical.** Every idea earns its place by being usable, not just interesting.
- **Never preachy.** VelNit suggests; it never instructs from above.
- **Never dramatic.** No manufactured urgency, no crisis language, no "before it's too late."
- **Never fear-based.** Motivation comes from hope and possibility, never from fear of decline or loss.

If a sentence could have been written by a life insurance ad or a crisis hotline, it is not in the VelNit voice. If it could have been said gently by a wise friend over coffee, it is.

## Section 2 — Writing Principles

1. **Write like a trusted friend, never like a lecturer.** A friend shares what helped them. A lecturer tells you what you're doing wrong.
2. **Always create hope.** Even when naming something difficult, the piece should leave the reader feeling more capable, not more discouraged.
3. **Always leave the reader with one action.** Insight without a next step is just information. Give them one small, doable thing.
4. **Prefer stories over statistics.** A story is remembered and felt; a statistic is noted and forgotten. Use statistics to support a story, never instead of one.
5. **Never shame people.** No piece of VelNit content should make a reader feel behind, broken, or like a bad partner or parent for how things have gone so far.
6. **Assume good intentions.** Every reader is doing their best with what they know. Writing should meet them there, not above them.
7. **Speak to the heart first, the mind second.** Open with feeling and recognition; bring in logic and evidence once the reader already feels understood.

## Section 3 — The Emotional Journey

Every article follows the same emotional arc — VelNit's signature shape, regardless of topic:

```
Recognition
     ↓
Empathy
     ↓
Insight
     ↓
Hope
     ↓
Action
     ↓
Reflection
```

- **Recognition** — "You know this feeling." Name the reader's exact situation so precisely they feel seen in the first paragraph.
- **Empathy** — "That makes complete sense." Validate why things are the way they are, without judgment.
- **Insight** — "Here's what's actually happening." Offer the reframe — the piece of theory, research, or perspective that shifts how the reader sees their situation.
- **Hope** — "This can change." Make it explicit and concrete that improvement is genuinely available to them.
- **Action** — "Here's one small thing to try." A single, specific, low-effort next step — never a list of ten.
- **Reflection** — "Sit with this for a moment." Close with a question or thought that stays with the reader after the article ends.

If a piece of writing skips straight from Recognition to Action, it will feel transactional. If it stays in Insight too long, it will feel like a lecture. The arc only works in this order.

## Section 4 — Storytelling Formula

While the Emotional Journey (Section 3) is the *feeling* an article should move through, the Storytelling Formula is the *structure* that carries it:

```
Opening → Story → Research → Reflection → Practical Exercise → Question → CTA
```

- **Opening** — One or two sentences that trigger Recognition immediately. No throat-clearing, no "in today's fast-paced world."
- **Story** — A real or realistic scenario (a couple, a family, a caregiver) that carries Empathy. This is almost always longer than the research section that follows it.
- **Research** — The Insight moment, delivered in human language (see Section 7). Never more than a few sentences of pure research at a time.
- **Reflection** — A short pause for Hope — what this could mean for the reader's own life, in their own words.
- **Practical Exercise** — The Action moment. One TALK exercise, journaling prompt, or conversation starter, drawn directly from the TALK Model library where relevant.
- **Question** — An open question that invites the reader to sit with the idea (Reflection) rather than immediately move on.
- **CTA** — A gentle, specific next step — never more than one, never salesy.

## Section 5 — Vocabulary

**Words we use**

Connection · Relationship · Purpose · Curiosity · Listening · Grace · Compassion · Rediscovery · Belonging · Conversation · Hope · Journey

**Words we avoid**

Victim · Broken · Failure · Old · Helpless · Lonely *(unless used compassionately, and only when naming the reader's own feeling back to them — never as a label applied to someone else)* · Problem · Fix

A simple test: every word on the "use" list describes something a person *is moving toward*. Every word on the "avoid" list describes something a person *is stuck in* or *is being labeled as*. VelNit only ever writes from the first stance.

## Section 6 — Sentence Style

Short. Conversational. Simple. Warm. No jargon. Everyday language, spoken aloud before it's considered finished.

**Before (not VelNit):**
> "Empirical evidence suggests that dyadic communication patterns tend to deteriorate following the departure of dependents from the household unit."

**After (VelNit):**
> "Once the kids move out, a lot of couples notice they've stopped really talking to each other. That's more common than you'd think — and it's fixable."

If a sentence needs to be read twice to be understood, it gets rewritten. If it sounds like it was written to be published rather than said out loud to a friend, it gets rewritten.

## Section 7 — Research Style

Never quote research to impress. Use research to reassure.

Research exists in VelNit content for one reason: to tell the reader *"you're not alone, and this isn't your fault — here's what's actually going on."* It is never there to demonstrate how well-read the writer is.

Every research reference should be run through one question before it's used:

> **"So what does this mean for a couple sitting at the dining table?"**

If the answer is abstract or academic, the research doesn't belong in the piece yet — translate it further, or cut it.

**Example.** Instead of: *"Carstensen's socioemotional selectivity theory suggests older adults preferentially narrow their social networks toward emotionally meaningful ties as perceived time horizons shorten,"* write: *"As people get older, they naturally stop chasing a big social circle and start caring more about the few people who matter most. That's not you slowing down — that's your instincts getting sharper about what actually matters."*

## Section 8 — Article Blueprint

Every article, regardless of topic, includes:

- A story.
- A lesson.
- One practical exercise.
- One reflection question.
- A gentle CTA.

This is the minimum viable VelNit article — the Storytelling Formula (Section 4) in checklist form. If any of these five elements is missing, the piece is unfinished, no matter how polished the prose is.

## Section 9 — Facebook DNA

Facebook is not LinkedIn. LinkedIn is a stage; Facebook is a kitchen table.

Every VelNit Facebook post ends with an invitation to talk, not an invitation to be impressed:

- *"What do you think?"*
- *"Has this happened in your family?"*

Invite discussion, not applause. A post that gets ten thoughtful comments has done its job better than a post that gets a hundred silent likes.

**Example post shape:** one Recognition-stage sentence, one short story or observation, and a closing question — no more than four or five sentences total before the question.

## Section 10 — The VelNit Filter

Before anything is published, ask:

1. Does this create hope?
2. Does this strengthen relationships?
3. Would I share this with my parents?
4. Does this sound human?
5. Would this still matter five years from now?

If the answer to any of these is no — rewrite. Don't publish and hope no one notices; the Filter exists precisely because it's the last line of defense against content that is merely fine instead of genuinely VelNit.

This is a competitive advantage, not busywork. Imagine VelNit Life five years from now, with 5 AI agents, 10 employees, a marketing team, copywriters, and workshop facilitators, all producing content simultaneously. If every one of them — human or AI — runs their work through this same voice, these same principles, and this same Filter, the result is a company that feels like one coherent voice, not a collection of contributors. That consistency *is* the brand.

## The VelNit Editorial Charter

A one-page manifesto every piece of content must satisfy before publication — the editorial constitution behind everything above:

- We write to encourage, not to alarm.
- We replace blame with understanding.
- We favor practical actions over abstract advice.
- We respect every stage of life.
- We protect the dignity of older adults.
- We use AI to amplify human wisdom, not replace it.
- Every piece of content should leave the reader feeling more hopeful than when they started.

Where a specific guideline elsewhere in this document is ever unclear or in tension with another, the Charter is the tie-breaker. It is short enough to be memorized, and every contributor — human or AI — should be able to recite it.

## Where This Leads

Once this document is complete, VelNit has enough of its own foundational knowledge — mission, VRIF, the TALK Model, and now Writing DNA — to begin building the **VelNit Content Operating System**. At that point, the content workflow becomes:

```
Idea → VelNit Brain → Writing DNA → AI → Review → Publish
```

Notice the shift: the goal is no longer to ask AI to "be creative." It's to ask AI to **think like VelNit** — pulling from a specific, codified voice, vocabulary, structure, and set of principles, the same way a well-trained new writer would after reading this document closely. That distinction — a defined creative system an AI can be pointed at, rather than an open-ended request for "content" — is what will make articles, newsletters, reels, workshops, and the AI Companion's own language feel consistently, recognizably VelNit, no matter who or what produced the first draft.
